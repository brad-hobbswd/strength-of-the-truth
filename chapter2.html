CHAPTER TWO

The Friend Who Spoke Up

Aaron showed up at David's door two days later, holding two cups of coffee and wearing an expression that made one thing clear. Aaron planned on staying awhile.

David sighed and stepped aside. The house looked tidy enough, yet silence filled every room with a heaviness that felt almost alive.

"You didn't have to come," David muttered as he lowered himself into a chair.

Aaron set coffee on the table and sat across from him. "I know." "I wanted to."

Aaron watched quietly for a moment. "You've been through a lot lately. The accident. Pressure from work. Tension at home. Yet you won't let anyone help."

David rubbed his forehead. "I'm handling things."

"No. You're surviving things. There's a difference."

Silence settled between them. Heavy. Uncomfortable. True.

David looked away. "I don't need pity." "I just need time."

Aaron's expression remained steady. "I'm not offering pity." "I'm offering truth." "You're hurting and pretending otherwise." "That doesn't make you stronger." "That isolates you."

David's chest tightened. "You think I don't know that?" "You think I don't feel that every night sitting alone?" "What am I supposed to do?" "Fall apart?"

Aaron leaned forward. "Maybe strength isn't pretending." "Maybe strength begins with honesty."

Those words cut through years of defenses David built around himself. Silence once felt like control. Silence once felt honorable. Now silence felt like another form of pain.

David finally whispered: "I don't even know where to start."

Aaron smiled softly. "Start by admitting you're not okay." "You don't need to fix everything today." "You only need to stop pretending."

As evening sunlight stretched across the room, David spoke words he had hidden for months.

"It hurts, Aaron." "Not only my side." "Everything."

Aaron nodded. "I know." "Open rebuke is better than secret love." "I care too much to watch you break."

Something inside David finally cracked open. Not weakness. Release. For the first time in months, truth sat openly in the room. For the first time in months, David no longer felt alone.
